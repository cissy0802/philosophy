DAY 50

Philosophy Classics: Critical Theory

July 10, 2026 · Exposing invisible domination, four voices
Critical Theory — When reason itself becomes an instrument of rule, how can thought stay critical?
The first 49 issues mostly asked "what is the world"; today asks "why is the world this way, and whose interest keeps it so." Critical Theory is not content to explain the world — it seeks to expose the structures that pose as natural but are really human domination: ideology, the culture industry, instrumental reason. It was born in the West (Marx lit the fuse, the Frankfurt School forged it), yet was seriously taken up and remade in East Asia. Today's four form a trans-continental dialogue: Marx unmasks alienated labor, Adorno declares enlightenment turned back into new myth, Li Zehou fuses Marx with Kant into a Chinese theory of "subjectivity," and Kojin Karatani restarts critique through "parallax." As issue 50, it forces a meta-question: is critique the super-individual's last freedom, or one more illusion?
Karl Marx
West · Historical Materialism (Alienation & Ideology)
1818–1883 · Economic and Philosophic Manuscripts of 1844; The German Ideology; Capital
Core Thesis + Primary Text
"In his labor the worker does not affirm himself but denies himself… he feels at home only outside his work, and in his work feels outside himself." — Economic and Philosophic Manuscripts of 1844, "Estranged Labour"
Historical Context & Core Insight

Marx faced an industrial-revolution Europe: machines roared, yet the worker grew poorer the more he produced, less himself the more he toiled. He named this alienation (Entfremdung) — the worker severed from his product, from his activity, even from his own humanity. Deeper still is ideology critique: the ruling ideas are merely the ruling class's interests in disguise; and commodity fetishism makes relations between people appear, invertedly, as relations between things. The eleventh Thesis on Feuerbach set the key for all of Critical Theory: "The philosophers have only interpreted the world in various ways; the point is to change it."

Cross-Disciplinary Link

Alienation takes a new form in the AI age: under algorithmic management, platform workers are dispatched, scored, and optimized by the system in real time — the tool comes to dominate the ones who built it. And "commodity fetishism" precisely foretold the reification of data: living human relations (likes, follows, ratings) are abstracted into tradable digital assets, and social relations once again appear as relations between things.

Contemporary Relevance
BigCat scenario: As AI takes over execution, beware a new alienation — the efficiency system you built yourself (KPIs, boards, automation pipelines) turns around to define and drive you. The super-individual's watershed: is the tool "for my use," or am I "in service to the tool"? Ask periodically: is this system expanding my freedom, or quietly setting my goals for me?
In a sentence: the deepest domination makes the dominated feel "this is just how it is" — the first step of critique is seeing that the "natural" is in fact "man-made."
Which "self-evident" rule or metric in your life was quietly written into your head by someone's interest?
Theodor Adorno (with Horkheimer)
West · Frankfurt School (Dialectic of Enlightenment)
1903–1969 · Dialectic of Enlightenment (with Horkheimer, 1947); Negative Dialectics
Core Thesis + Primary Text
"Enlightenment has always aimed at liberating men from fear and making them masters. Yet the fully enlightened earth radiates disaster triumphant." — Dialectic of Enlightenment
Historical Context & Core Insight

Having lived through Nazism and exile, Adorno faced a piercing question: why did the most rational Europe give birth to Auschwitz? His answer, with Horkheimer, was that enlightenment reason had long narrowed into instrumental reason: it asks only "how to attain the goal efficiently," never "whether the goal itself is right." It conquered nature and ended by turning humans into calculable, manageable objects. Its most everyday incarnation is the culture industry (Kulturindustrie): popular culture mass-produced on an assembly line, pacifying the masses with a standardized "pseudo-individuality" — you think you are freely choosing, while consuming what was designed in advance.

Cross-Disciplinary Link

"The culture industry" is almost an exact prophecy of the recommendation algorithm and the attention economy. Today's feeds tout personalization with "a face for every viewer," yet underneath runs the same standardized logic of maximizing dwell time — Adorno's "pseudo-individuality": the difference is on the surface, the manipulation uniform. And "instrumental reason" is the very temperament of large models: the most powerful "how" engine ever built, silent on the "why."

Contemporary Relevance
BigCat scenario: Beware the ideology of "personalization." What the recommender gives you is not what you truly want, but what best keeps you. Actively curate an information diet for yourself and your child — deliberately read what the algorithm would never surface. Same for AI products: the metric you can optimize ≠ the goal worth optimizing; take the "why" back into your own hands.
In a sentence: hyper-efficient instrumental reason answers every "how" yet is blind to "why" — and wisdom lives precisely in the "why."
Of the content you scrolled yesterday, how much did you "choose," and how much did the algorithm "choose for you"?
Li Zehou
East · Chinese Marxism (Subjectivity · Sedimentation)
1930–2021 · A Critique of Critical Philosophy: A Commentary on Kant (1979); Essays on Modern Chinese Intellectual History
Core Thesis + Primary Text
"By 'sedimentation' I mean: the rational sediments into the sensuous, the social into the individual, the historical into the psyche." — Li Zehou, on "anthropological-historical ontology"
Historical Context & Core Insight

Li Zehou was one of twentieth-century China's most influential philosophers. He fused Marx's "practice" with Kant's "subject": to be human begins with the collective historical practice of "making and using tools"; and the fruits of that long practice "sediment" into an individual's psyche and sensibility — what you find beautiful or right today is, in fact, millennia of history quietly sunk into your heart. He further inverted Zhang Zhidong's "Chinese essence, Western use" into "Western essence, Chinese use": take the modern (Marx included) as "essence," Chinese tradition as "use." This is a genuine non-Western remaking of Critical Theory.

Cross-Disciplinary Link

"Sedimentation" chimes with gene-culture coevolution: culture is not merely external information — it settles into the individual's cognitive and emotional structure. It also fits neuroscience: the "prior framework" through which we perceive the world is not innately fixed but long shaped by history and culture. This echoes Gadamer in Day 49: what he called "prejudice" is exactly Li Zehou's "sedimentation."

Contemporary Relevance
BigCat scenario: Parenting is essentially "sedimentation" — the daily life, language, and aesthetics you give a child sink into the bedrock of their psyche and become their future "unthinking" judgments. It also marks the human–AI divide: a large model has no sedimentation, only weights; human judgment comes from the depth of layered historical experience — a moat that cannot be "trained" in one pass.
In a sentence: your "intuition" is not from nowhere — it is history and culture sedimented within you; only by seeing this can you speak of true autonomy.
The aesthetic or judgment you most take to be "instinct" — trace its source: which stretch of history and culture sedimented it into you?
Kojin Karatani
East · Japanese Critical Theory (Transcritique · Parallax)
1941– · Transcritique: On Kant and Marx (2001); The Structure of World History (2010)
Core Thesis + Primary Text
"The critique produced by constantly moving between Kant and Marx I call 'transcritique.' Truth appears only in the 'parallax' between two incommensurable systems." — Transcritique
Historical Context & Core Insight

Karatani is contemporary East Asia's most important critical theorist. He observed: every single system has its own invisible blind spot, and you cannot critique it from inside. Real critique requires "parallax" — just as two offset eyes generate depth, you must move back and forth between two mutually incompatible standpoints (Kant's ethics vs Marx's political economy) for the blind spot to surface. In later work he replaced "mode of production" with "modes of exchange" (reciprocity / plunder-and-redistribution / commodity exchange / and an X beyond the three), imagining a path beyond capitalism.

Cross-Disciplinary Link

"Parallax" is structurally identical to Bohr's complementarity in quantum mechanics: an electron's wave and particle natures cannot appear in the same experiment; you can only switch between mutually exclusive setups to piece together the full picture. Truth is not within any one perspective, but in the gap between perspectives. This too answers Day 49's hermeneutics: since a "prejudice-free God's-eye view" does not exist, the alternative to objectivity is Karatani's deliberate movement among multiple standpoints.

Contemporary Relevance
BigCat scenario: Don't let major decisions spin inside a single frame. Deliberately create "parallax" — on the same question, force yourself to first stand in capital's view, then the user's; first be a tech optimist, then a pessimist. Have AI argue opposing sides for you; what you want is not one side's answer, but the structural blind spot exposed in the gap between the two.
In a sentence: from inside a system you cannot see its walls; real critique moves between two incompatible standpoints and lets the blind spot reveal itself.
Your most firmly held judgment right now — if you forced yourself to argue for its opposite, what previously invisible thing would you see?
Four Views of Critique in Chorus Synthesis
Marx · Adorno · Li Zehou · Kojin Karatani
Chorus

Four thinkers spanning Eurasia and two centuries answer one question — how can domination that poses as "natural" be seen, and then loosened?
· Marx (alienation): the worker denies himself in his labor, and ruling ideas are ruling interests in disguise — critique begins by seeing that "just how it is" is man-made.
· Adorno (negation): enlightenment narrows into instrumental reason, and the culture industry pacifies with mass-produced "pseudo-individuality" — a reason that answers every "how" and no "why" is the new myth.
· Li Zehou (sedimentation): the rational sediments into the sensuous, the historical into the psyche — your "intuition" is history written into you, and seeing this is the precondition of autonomy.
· Karatani (parallax): from inside a system its walls are invisible; truth appears only in the gap between two incompatible standpoints.
Used together they form an "operating system for critique": use Marx for the interest audit — of every "self-evident" rule, first ask whose interest keeps it so; use Adorno to reclaim the why — for every optimizable metric, ask who set this "why," and take the goal back from the tool; use Li Zehou for self-archaeology — treat your "unthinking" judgments as historical sediment, sorting what deserves inheriting from what needs updating; use Karatani for the parallax drill — force major judgments to shuttle between opposed standpoints until the blind spot surfaces in the gap. The AI era presses all four dominations at once: algorithmic management is the new alienation, the feed is the culture industry perfected, large models have weights but no sedimentation, and the filter bubble is precisely the death of parallax. Critique is not cynicism; it is the super-individual's first capacity for winning clarity amid encircling systems — see through first, then decide: use it, reform it, or route around it.

Reflection
Pick one daily arrangement you have never questioned — a KPI, a house rule, an information source — and run it through the four stations: whose interest? who set the "why"? what sedimented history makes it feel natural? what does it look like from the opposite standpoint? After the run, would you still carry it out unchanged?
DEEP REFLECTION
1. Li Zehou inverted Zhang Zhidong's "Chinese essence, Western use" into "Western essence, Chinese use" — was Critical Theory's eastward passage "importing the West into China" or "sinicizing the West"?
Neither, quite. Li Zehou and Karatani show that East Asia did not passively receive Marx but "remade" him from its own situation — Li fused Kantian subjectivity and Confucian "emotion" into historical materialism; Karatani rewrote world history via "modes of exchange." This shows the core of Critical Theory is no Western monopoly but a universal impulse: to expose domination and imagine another possibility; it grows into whatever form its cultural soil allows. East Asia could catch Marx perhaps because Confucianism already carried a worldly, critical gene of "taking all-under-heaven as one's own charge."
2. Karatani says real critique must stand outside the system — but Day 49's Gadamer says a "prejudice-free external standpoint" does not exist at all. How to reconcile?
Karatani does not claim a pure "God's-eye view outside the system" — that is exactly what he denies. His method is "parallax," not transcendence: you need no nonexistent neutral point, only two standpoints, each with its own prejudice, mutually incompatible; moving between them, the blind spot surfaces in the gap. This completes Gadamer neatly: since prejudice cannot be abolished, use one prejudice to illuminate what another cannot see. Objectivity is not "having no standpoint," but "having and switching among several."
3. Critical Theory excels at "exposing domination" yet is often mocked as "all demolition, no construction" — in the AI age, what constructive proposal can it offer after critique?
This is Critical Theory's truest soft spot: the Frankfurt School grew ever more pessimistic, and Adorno was left with almost nothing but negation. But Karatani's "mode of exchange X" and Habermas's "communicative rationality" are both turns toward construction. For the super-individual, critique's value is not overturning everything but winning clarity for action: first see through a system's ideology, then decide to "use it, reform it, or route around it." Demolition serves more accurate building — construction without critique often just repackages the old domination in prettier wrapping.
