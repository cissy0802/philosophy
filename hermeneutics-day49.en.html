DAY 49

Philosophy Classics: Hermeneutics

July 8, 2026 · How understanding is possible, four voices
Hermeneutics — When you read a text or grasp a person, are you recovering an original meaning, or generating a new one?
Hermeneutics asks about an act we perform every day yet never think through: understanding. Reading a classic, hearing out a subordinate, reading a child's eyes, even reading a stretch of AI output — where does meaning come from? Today's four thinkers line up as an East–West matrix. The text-centered pole: Dilthey legislates for "understanding," setting it apart from natural-scientific "explanation"; Zhu Xi urges "I annotate the Classics" — glossing word by word, immersing with an empty mind, letting the original meaning surface. The reader-centered pole: Gadamer inverts the Enlightenment, declaring that "prejudice" is the precondition of understanding, which is a "fusion of horizons"; Lu Xiangshan goes further — "the Classics annotate me," mere footnotes to awaken one's own mind. Recover the original, or generate the new? This tension runs across Eurasia.
Wilhelm Dilthey
West · Hermeneutics of Life (Understanding vs Explanation)
1833–1911 · Ideas Concerning a Descriptive and Analytic Psychology (1894); Introduction to the Human Sciences (1883)
Core Thesis + Primary Text
"Nature we explain; the life of the mind we understand." (Die Natur erklären wir, das Seelenleben verstehen wir.) — Ideas
Historical Context & Core Insight

In the nineteenth century, positivism (Comte) was ascendant, bent on folding history and psychology into natural science and explaining everything by causal law. Dilthey drew a border: the natural sciences seek causal explanation (Erklären) — subsuming phenomena from outside under general laws; the human sciences (Geisteswissenschaften) seek understanding of meaning (Verstehen) — re-living (Nacherleben) another's expressed life from within. A letter, a legal code, are "objectifications of life"; understanding passes through these traces back to the living experience poured into them. Hermeneutics thereby rose from a technique of exegesis to the methodological foundation of the human sciences.

Cross-Disciplinary Link

"Explanation vs understanding" prefigures today's dispute over meaning in AI. Large models excel at Erklären — extracting statistical correlations from vast corpora, predicting the next word. But do they possess Verstehen — a first-person grasp of meaning? Searle's "Chinese Room" is precisely the modern form of this line: however fluent the symbol-shuffling, it is not the same as grasping. Dilthey pointed it out a century early: predicting a behavior and understanding what it means to the one who acts are two different things.

Contemporary Relevance
BigCat scenario: Split the AI–human division of labor along this line — let AI do the Erklären work (data patterns, trend forecasts, code completion), and keep Verstehen for yourself (why a report is losing morale, a client's real worry, the emotion behind a child's silence). The stronger AI grows, the more you should shift energy from "explaining the world" toward "understanding people" — the part it cannot replace.
In a sentence: to understand is not to subsume a person under laws, but to live through them once more — the bottom line of what makes the humanities human.
The last time you truly "understood" someone — did you analyze them, or briefly become them?
Hans-Georg Gadamer
West · Philosophical Hermeneutics (Fusion of Horizons)
1900–2002 · Truth and Method (Wahrheit und Methode, 1960)
Core Thesis + Primary Text
"The prejudices of the individual, far more than his judgments, constitute the historical reality of his being." (Die Vorurteile des Einzelnen sind weit mehr als seine Urteile die geschichtliche Wirklichkeit seines Seins.) — Truth and Method
Historical Context & Core Insight

Gadamer inverts the Enlightenment's deepest dogma — "to understand objectively, first sweep away all prejudice (Vorurteil)." He counters: a mind with no prejudice, language, or tradition has no way even to begin understanding. Prejudice is not error but the fore-structure that lets understanding start. So understanding is no longer bowing down to recover an author's "original meaning," but the interpreter's horizon meeting and merging with the text's horizon, generating a larger new one — this is the "fusion of horizons" (Horizontverschmelzung). By it he challenges Dilthey: meaning does not wait in the past to be dug up, but grows in present dialogue. Truth overflows method.

Cross-Disciplinary Link

"Prejudice precedes understanding" is strikingly isomorphic with neuroscience's predictive processing. The brain does not passively receive sensory data; it constantly uses a prior model to actively predict input, sending back only the error for correction — perception has been called a "controlled hallucination." There is no "prejudice-free" pure seeing: whether looking at the world or reading a text, we always enter with a prior framework in place. Gadamer's philosophical thesis is exactly the first-person version of this cognitive mechanism.

Contemporary Relevance
BigCat scenario: A dialogue with AI is essentially a fusion of horizons. You ask (prompt) carrying your prejudices; a good exchange is not one-way "answer extraction" but growing, in the back-and-forth, a judgment you didn't have before. Likewise, understanding a child is not emptying yourself for "objective observation," but owning your horizon and letting it genuinely meet the child's — projection distorts, but pretending to have no horizon is equally self-deceit.
In a sentence: there is no "prejudice-free understanding"; the honest path is not to abolish bias but to let it be tested and widened in dialogue.
Your most solid "prejudice" — is it a lens for understanding the world, or a wall that keeps it out? How would you tell?
Zhu Xi (Huian)
East · Song Neo-Confucianism ("I annotate the Classics")
1130–1200 · Collected Commentaries on the Four Books; Classified Conversations of Master Zhu · On Reading
Core Thesis + Primary Text
"The method of reading lies in proceeding step by step, reading thoroughly and pondering finely." And: "With an empty mind, steep and immerse; bring it home and examine it against yourself." — Classified Conversations · On Reading
Historical Context & Core Insight

Zhu Xi faced a Song vogue for doubting and rewriting the Classics, and a Chan-style groundless sudden enlightenment — everyone presuming direct access to the sages' mind. He devoted his life to annotating the Four Books, advocating a text-centered reading: first philology (fix the exact sense of each word), then phrasing (sort out the syntax), and only then dare seek the moral meaning. The core discipline is the "empty mind" — set aside your bias, let the text speak for itself, rather than "first fixing your own view, then using the book as proof." This chimes across the world with Schleiermacher and Dilthey's objective hermeneutics: both trust that rigorous philological method can approach the author's original meaning.

Cross-Disciplinary Link

Zhu Xi's "empty mind" forms an exact counter-thesis to Gadamer. Where Gadamer reveals that "prejudice is unavoidable," Zhu Xi offers a discipline for restraining prejudice: knowing you carry bias, still grind away partiality through "immersion" and "self-examination." The two need not conflict — Gadamer describes the existential structure of understanding, Zhu Xi the cultivation method of reading; one states "how it is," the other prescribes "how it should be."

Contemporary Relevance
BigCat scenario: The scarcest ability in the AI age is "slow reading of first-hand texts." Second-hand takes and AI summaries are everywhere at hand — which is exactly why Zhu Xi's discipline of "word by word, immersing with an empty mind" becomes a moat: real understanding cannot be outsourced. Reading original philosophy, a key contract, your team's raw data — all deserve Zhu Xi's slow labor: first grasp what it says, then ask what it means for you.
In a sentence: let the text speak first, then speak yourself — "empty mind" is not having no stance, but pawning your stance until it is tested, then reclaiming it.
Your last important judgment — was it based on reading first-hand material, or on conclusions someone (or an AI) had already chewed for you?
Lu Jiuyuan (Xiangshan)
East · School of Mind ("The Classics annotate me")
1139–1193 · Collected Works of Lu Jiuyuan · Recorded Sayings; the 1175 "Goose Lake Meeting" debate with Zhu Xi
Core Thesis + Primary Text
"Someone asked the Master: why don't you write books? He answered: The Six Classics annotate me, and I annotate the Six Classics." And: "If in learning one knows the root, the Six Classics are all my footnotes." — Recorded Sayings
Historical Context & Core Insight

At the 1175 Goose Lake Meeting, Lu Xiangshan and Zhu Xi clashed face to face. Zhu Xi held "investigate things to exhaust principle" — principle lies outside, to be sought in the Classics' words; Xiangshan held "the mind is principle" — principle is already complete within the human mind; reading the Classics is not extracting meaning from outside but confirming and awakening the heavenly principle already in the mind. Hence "the Classics annotate me": the Classics are footnotes serving "my" awakening, not an authority I must bow beneath. This is a radical reader-centered stance — meaning's home is the reader's own mind, not the letter of the text.

Cross-Disciplinary Link

"The Classics annotate me" is almost the Eastern forerunner of Gadamer's "fusion of horizons," and runs straight to modern reader-response theory: a text's meaning is activated and completed in the reader's situation. So the four thinkers form a clear matrix — Dilthey / Zhu Xi guard the "text pole," Gadamer / Lu Jiuyuan the "reader pole." One and the same tension: Eurasia, independently, walked to both ends.

Contemporary Relevance
BigCat scenario: The "super-individual" is not a stack of knowledge but integration and internalization. Every external input — books, reports, an AI's answers — should ultimately "annotate me": serve your own judgment, or else it is just a mobile hard drive. But Xiangshan left a warning too: cut loose from Zhu Xi's philological grounding, "the Classics annotate me" easily degrades into self-conceit, mistaking bias for insight. The real skill is using both poles — able to let material speak with an empty mind, and able to make it finally serve you.
In a sentence: reading ends in "annotating me" — refining borrowed knowledge into your own judgment; but without the grounding of "annotating the Classics," "annotating me" is mere conceit.
When you digest new knowledge, do you let it remake you, or only pick out the parts that confirm what you already thought?
DEEP REFLECTION
1. Dilthey/Zhu Xi (text pole) and Gadamer/Lu Jiuyuan (reader pole) line up as a matrix — is this East–West symmetry a coincidence?
Most likely not coincidence, but a consequence of the structure of the act of "understanding" itself. Any understanding happens between "text" and "reader"; which pole to weight toward is a limited choice. That thinkers across Eurasia, with no contact, each walked to the two ends strongly suggests: the tension of objective recovery vs subjective generation is the universal skeleton of interpretation, not one culture's specialty. The difference: the West raised it to a dispute of methodology and ontology; the East settled it in the cultivation ethics of "reading discipline" and "the original mind."
2. "I annotate the Classics" and "the Classics annotate me" — can they be reconciled, or must one choose a side?
A mature reader is in fact two-staged: first "I annotate the Classics" — empty-minded philology, grasping what the text actually says, restraining projection; then "the Classics annotate me" — internalizing the grasped into one's own judgment. "Annotating me" that skips the first step is self-conceit; stopping at the first step is a mobile hard drive. Zhu Xi's dispute with Xiangshan is less right-versus-wrong than which half of the same process to weight — and the real skill is knowing which half this moment calls for.
3. Ricoeur said "once written, a text detaches from its author and opens to all readers" — what does this do to "authorial intent"?
Ricoeur's "textual autonomy" (distanciation) stands between the two poles: he rejects Dilthey's fantasy of "climbing back into the author's mind," yet does not license arbitrary distortion. Once a text leaves its author it becomes an independent meaning-structure; the reader faces a "world of the text," not the "author's psychology." This is especially apt in the AI age: text generated by a large model has no "authorial intent" to trace back to at all — you can only reason from the text itself. Yet this does not mean anything goes: the text still draws borders.
4. Can AI "understand," or is it forever stuck at what Dilthey called "explanation"?
For Dilthey, the core of Verstehen is "re-living (Nacherleben)" — it needs a subject with first-person experience to "live through another once." Current large models have no experience; strictly they have only Erklären (statistical correlation and prediction). But the border is blurring: when a model can stably model others' intentions, predict emotion, and generate fitting responses, at the functional level it becomes hard to distinguish from "understanding." The real question may not be "does it grasp," but whether "grasping" is an inner experience or an externally displayable capacity — where hermeneutics meets the philosophy of consciousness.
